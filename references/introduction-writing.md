# Introduction Writing

## Purpose

Build a Management Science-style introduction that sells the research question to a busy editor while preserving the formal contribution.

## When to Use

Use when drafting, revising, or critiquing an introduction, contribution block, abstract-adjacent overview, or paper roadmap.

## Inputs Needed

- Real-world setting and decision maker.
- Decision variables studied in the paper.
- Central trade-off and concrete example.
- Closest prior work and restrictive assumptions.
- Research question.
- Main analytical answer and contribution list.

## Procedure

Use this narrative architecture:

1. Context. Open with the practitioner-facing setting. End by naming every decision object the paper studies.
2. Trade-off. Use one grounded example, preferably with numbers, to show both extremes and why neither is enough.
3. Difficulty. Explain why the problem is hard on its own terms. Do not preview the answer here.
4. Research question. Name closest prior work, state what it assumes and proves, identify the restrictive assumption, and ask one interrogative research question.
5. Managerial answer. Answer at a plain-language level. Say what the paper means before saying what it proves.
6. Contributions block. Use four items when possible: model or representation, main structural theorem and robustness, optimization or prescription, numerical evidence.
7. Roadmap. Use one sentence per section.

## Expected Output

Return either a paragraph-by-paragraph outline or polished prose. Also provide a promise audit:

- Are all decision variables introduced early?
- Does P2 show the trade-off?
- Does P4 frame a new question instead of a small extension?
- Does P5 avoid duplicating the formal contributions?
- Does every contribution have support in the body?

## Common Failure Modes

- Opening with technical definitions.
- Introducing a decision variable only in the contribution block.
- Saying "we extend" when the paper can be framed as a new research question.
- Previewing the answer before explaining the difficulty.
- Mixing the managerial answer and theorem-level contribution.
- Using contribution verbs such as "explore" or "discuss" when the paper proves or characterizes.
