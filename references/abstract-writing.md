# Journal-Aware Abstract Writing

## Purpose

Draft or revise an OR/MS abstract whose structure fits the target journal and whose claims remain traceable to the manuscript.

## Select the Journal Profile

Identify the target journal before choosing a structure. Follow user-supplied or verified current journal instructions when they are available.

- For **Manufacturing & Service Operations Management (MSOM)**, use the labeled blocks `Problem Definition`, `Methodology/Results`, and `Managerial Implications`. When submission compliance matters, verify the current labels and word limit rather than relying on memory.
- For **Management Science**, **Operations Research**, another journal, or an unspecified journal, default to a conventional unlabeled abstract unless the user or current journal instructions require another format.
- Do not transfer the MSOM block structure to another journal merely because the paper is in OM/OR or seeks a Management Science-style audience.

## Build a Sentence-Role Map

Write from the stable body, results, and evidence when possible. Assign each sentence one primary role; a sentence may also support a secondary role. Combine or omit roles when the paper does not need all of them.

1. **Managerial setting or decision:** establish the real decision and why it matters.
2. **Core tension:** state the competing forces that make the decision difficult.
3. **Gap or research question:** identify what existing approaches do not resolve, without asserting an open problem unless the literature supports that claim.
4. **Approach:** state the model, method, or design idea in plain language.
5. **Headline results:** report the strongest supported theoretical, computational, or empirical findings.
6. **Benchmark and scope:** make comparison bases, assumptions, qualifiers, and validity boundaries visible.
7. **Managerial meaning:** explain what the findings change about decisions, actions, or risk.

For an MSOM abstract, place the setting, tension, and question in `Problem Definition`; the approach, results, benchmark, and scope in `Methodology/Results`; and supported decision meaning in `Managerial Implications`. For an unlabeled abstract, arrange the same logic as one continuous argument.

## Draft and Audit

- Preserve every material assumption, scope restriction, calibration condition, and theorem qualifier needed to keep a claim true.
- Keep the benchmark visible. A guarantee or improvement is incomplete if the comparison basis is unclear.
- Tie each numerical claim to its metric and baseline. Do not combine results from different settings as though they were directly comparable.
- Distinguish proved results from numerical evidence, design motivation, interpretation, and conjecture.
- Do not claim novelty, an open problem, generality, deployability, or practical superiority beyond the manuscript and literature evidence.
- Prefer plain language over undefined notation and specialized jargon, but do not simplify away load-bearing conditions.
- Check that the managerial implication follows from the reported evidence rather than from aspiration.
- Count words under the selected journal's convention and report uncertainty when the counting rule is not known.

## Expected Output

Return:

1. A brief journal-profile note stating the selected structure and whether current submission rules were verified.
2. A compact sentence-role map.
3. The polished abstract.
4. The word count and counting convention, if known.
5. Concise claim-evidence warnings for unsupported, overstated, or underspecified statements.
